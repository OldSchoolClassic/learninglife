Getting Started
How do I get started with homeschooling?

Starting to homeschool — at the beginning of the year or partway through — is completely doable, and Pennsylvania has a clear legal process.

Important: You do not need your district's approval to begin homeschooling. You may begin immediately. You must have beginning-of-year documentation submitted within 10 days of beginning.

If starting before August 1: Submit to your superintendent: a notarized affidavit (or unsworn declaration), a list of educational objectives, and a Medical Services & Dental record or exemption statement. Deadline: August 1.

If starting mid-year: Follow the same steps, withdraw your child from their current school, and start keeping a portfolio from day one. Save samples of prior school work to establish a baseline.

See the Start of Year Forms page for all templates and downloads.

In which counties or districts do you conduct evaluations?

All PA counties! Since I conduct all evaluations virtually via Google Meet, I can serve families statewide.

What qualifies you to evaluate homeschool programs?

Under Pennsylvania law, homeschool evaluators must have at least two years of teaching experience at the appropriate grade level. I hold:

  • A valid PA PK-4 (elementary) certification
  • A valid PA PK-12 special education certification
  • Full-time classroom experience at the elementary and high school levels
  • 7+ years tutoring high school students with various needs

This qualifies me to evaluate all grade levels K–12, including students with active IEPs, nonverbal students, unschoolers, and those using non-traditional approaches.

The Evaluation
What does a homeschool evaluation look like?

All evaluations are conducted virtually via Google Meet. Simply click the link in your confirmation email at your appointment time.

A typical evaluation is a relaxed, encouraging conversation about your child's growth over the year. I'll review your portfolio samples, ask your child a few simple questions, and discuss how the year went — celebrating progress and answering your questions. Most evaluations last 30–60 minutes per child.

Afterward, I'll email you the signed evaluation form with instructions for submitting it to your superintendent or umbrella school.

What should I bring to the evaluation?

Please bring: samples from each required subject (workbook pages, writing samples, project photos, etc.); a reading log; standardized assessment results (3rd, 5th, and 8th grade only); and anything else showing progress. The goal is to demonstrate consistent, sustained education — not perfection.

See the full Preparing for Your Evaluation page for a detailed checklist.

Does my child need to attend the evaluation?

Yes. PA law requires a student interview during the evaluation. However, your child does not need to stay for the entire duration — just the brief interview portion.

What questions are asked during the student interview?

The student portion is informal and tailored to their age. Sample questions include:

  1. Walk me through a typical day in your homeschool.
  2. What is something you remember learning this year?
  3. Which subject do you want to tell me about first?
  4. Tell me about a book you read this year.
  5. Describe a time when you were really interested in or excited about something you were learning.
  6. What kinds of things have you been practicing or exploring?
  7. What's a topic or subject you'd be happy to keep learning more about?

I encourage parents to review the portfolio with their child beforehand so they feel comfortable and excited to share their work!

My child is shy or nervous about the interview.

That's completely okay! Many kids — especially younger ones or first-timers — feel unsure about talking to someone new. I keep the student portion relaxed and friendly — more like a conversation than an interview.

I'll ask gentle, open-ended questions and let your child share at their own pace. They're welcome to show me their work, tell a story, or even just nod or point. There's no pressure to give perfect answers. I'm not testing your child — I'm simply getting a glimpse into how they spent their year learning and growing.

Most kids end up enjoying it once they realize it's not a big deal!

I'm nervous — what if we didn't do enough?

You're not alone. Many parents feel nervous the first time (or even the fifth!). Please know that I approach evaluations with encouragement, not judgment. My role is to help you meet legal requirements while celebrating your child's progress.

You don't need a perfect year or a polished portfolio. I'm looking for meaningful learning and growth that makes sense for your child and your family situation.

Remember, I'm a homeschooling mom too. Our family has gone through seasons that made homeschooling difficult — job changes, financial stress, moving, new babies, health issues, and more. I know it can feel like something is always getting in the way. If you're unsure whether you've "done enough," we'll talk through it together. You're probably doing more than you think.

What happens if I turn in my evaluation after June 30?

Technically you are out of compliance if you miss the June 30 deadline. In practice, your district will typically contact you (by phone or letter) requesting the evaluation before taking further action.

As long as you respond and submit your evaluation, it is unlikely to escalate. Keeping open communication with your district about when you'll submit goes a long way. For families that are genuinely homeschooling, severe consequences are rare when the situation is handled proactively.

Nonverbal Children & Special Needs
How can nonverbal children be interviewed?

PA evaluations require a student interview — but this does not require the child to speak. For nonverbal students, the interview can take the form of observation, parent-facilitated interaction, or use of AAC devices. I may watch the student engage with materials, respond through gestures or body language, or communicate via tablets or communication boards.

Parents can also share videos or explain how the child interacts and learns. The goal is simply to respectfully acknowledge the student as an active learner and document their progress.

Please let me know ahead of time if your child is nonverbal so I can prepare accordingly.

Do you evaluate students with special needs?

Yes! Students with special needs make up a significant portion of homeschooled students, and I hold a valid PA PK-12 special education certification. I'm qualified to evaluate students with active IEPs at all grade levels.

I focus on progress that is appropriate for your child — not grade-level comparisons. See the IEP Students page for more detail.

Do you evaluate unschoolers or families without formal curriculum?

Yes! Your method of homeschooling is your choice. The law stipulates required subjects — not how they must be taught. Documentation for unschoolers might look different but absolutely works:

  • Photos of projects, activities, or outings
  • Videos of presentations, recitals, or instrument practice
  • Creative writing samples, drawings, artwork
  • Screenshots or certificates from educational apps or digital games
  • Weekly summaries of learning activities written by the parent or child

It doesn't have to be exhaustive — just enough to show that learning happened consistently.

Portfolios & Records
How long do I need to save previous years' portfolios?

There is no official PA law on portfolio retention.

  • Grades K–6: I recommend saving 2–3 years worth, in case your program is ever questioned.
  • Grades 7–12: Save all portfolios at least until your child has their diploma. Some sub-subjects required for graduation can count if covered in 7th or 8th grade.

You don't need to keep everything — just the portfolio (samples, reading log, assessment results, and evaluation form).

Standardized Assessments
What do I need to know about standardized assessments?

PA law (24 P.S. § 13-1327.1) requires standardized assessment results for 3rd, 5th, and 8th grade only. Results for other grades are not required.

You may use PSSAs (through your district) or any of these state-approved nationally normed tests:

  1. California Achievement Test (recommended — available online, parent-administered, reasonably priced)
  2. Comprehensive Testing Program (CTPIV)
  3. Iowa Test of Basic Skills
  4. Measures of Academic Progress (MAP)
  5. Metropolitan Achievement Test
  6. Peabody Achievement Individual Test – Revised
  7. Stanford Achievement Test
  8. Terra Nova
  9. Woodcock-Johnson Revised Tests of Achievement
  10. Woodcock-Johnson
  11. Wechsler Individual Achievement Test

There is no minimum passing score. Assessment scores are not a primary factor in my evaluations — it's simply a requirement to check off. Students may take the test at their current academic level, not necessarily their registered grade level.

If your child was evaluated for special needs during a required grade year, they may have already taken an approved test. Check before scheduling additional testing.

Graduation & Diplomas
What should I know about 12th grade evaluations?

12th grade evaluations are similar in format to other years but carry additional weight. If you're issuing a parent diploma, I must confirm that your student has completed all required high school subjects:

  • 4 years of English (including language, literature, speech, and composition)
  • 3 years of Math (including general math, algebra, and geometry)
  • 3 years of Science
  • 3 years of Social Studies (including civics, U.S. & PA history, and world history)
  • 2 years of Arts and Humanities
  • Plus: geography, art, music, PE, health, and safety education at some point in grades 7–12

Sub-subjects can count as early as 7th grade — so save portfolios starting in 7th grade. Please bring transcripts and/or portfolio samples for all secondary required subjects.

Can my child graduate early?

Yes! Under PA homeschool law, students may graduate before age 18 if they've completed all required subjects. Courses completed in grades 7 and 8 can count toward graduation requirements.

I'm happy to review your records and help confirm that graduation requirements have been met. Reach out to discuss.

Is a parent-issued diploma valid?

Yes. A change in PA home education law in October 2014 established the parent-issued diploma as a fully state-recognized option. When it meets PA requirements and is signed by a qualified 12th-grade evaluator, it carries the same rights and privileges as an organization-issued diploma — including for college applications, scholarships, and employment.

HSLDA advocates strongly for these rights if any issues arise. See the Graduation & Diplomas page for full details.

Do you work for the state?

No. Homeschool evaluators are not contracted or employed by the state of Pennsylvania. I operate independently, and my evaluations reflect my personal philosophy: your children are yours, not the state's.

I conduct evaluations with legal integrity — upholding what the law requires — without giving the state any additional information beyond what is legally necessary. I'm on your side, not theirs.
